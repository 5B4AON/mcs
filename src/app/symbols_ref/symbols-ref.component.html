1. International Morse Code

International Morse Code encodes each character as a unique sequence of short signals (dits, written as .) and long signals (dahs, written as -). A dah is three times the length of a dit. Characters are separated by a gap equal to three dits, and words are separated by a gap equal to seven dits.

2. Prosigns (Procedural Signals)

2.1 What Are Prosigns?

Prosigns are special Morse sequences where two or more letters are sent together without the normal inter-character gap, forming a single symbol with a unique meaning. They are written with an overline (e.g., AR) or in angle brackets (<AR>) and act as procedural commands during a CW QSO.

Some prosigns share their Morse pattern with a punctuation character — for example <AR> and + are both .-.-.. Morse Code Studio can optionally display the prosign name instead of the punctuation mark for improved clarity; this is controlled by the Show Prosigns setting.

4. Q-Codes

4.1 What Are Q-Codes?

Q-codes are three-letter codes beginning with “Q” originally developed for commercial radiotelegraph communication. Each Q-code serves as a shorthand for a complete question or statement. When sent with a question mark, the code asks a question; without the question mark, it is a statement or answer.

For example, QTH? means “What is your location?” while QTH followed by a place name means “My location is…”

5. 92 Code (Numbered Codes)

5.1 What Is the 92 Code?

The 92 Code is a set of numbered brevity codes adopted by Western Union in 1859. These shorthand numeric abbreviations were used in CW and radiotelegraph to convey common phrases, reducing bandwidth consumption over telegraph lines. Several codes — most notably 73 and 88 — remain in widespread amateur radio use today.

5.2 Common 92 Codes

CodeMeaning
22Love and kisses (less common variant of 88)
33Fondest regards (between YLs — female operators)
44Best wishes / WX (weather-related acknowledgement)
55Best success / good luck
72Best regards (QRP community greeting)
73Best regards — universal ham radio farewell
88Love and kisses — affectionate farewell
99Go away / Get lost (impolite dismissal)
Note: 73 is by far the most commonly used numbered code. It is customary to send it at the end of a QSO. “73” is already plural — saying “73s” is technically redundant but widely tolerated. 88 is often appended when conversing with an YL or XYL, or in a light-hearted context.
