2.1  How the Decoder Works

The decoder listens for key-down / key-up timing events from every enabled input source and converts the resulting dit-dah patterns into text. Decoded characters appear in the Morse Decoder panel.

Each incoming interval (key-press duration or silence gap) is measured in milliseconds and classified as a dit, dah, intra-character gap, inter-character gap or word space using adaptive timing thresholds.

2.2  Dual Calibration — RX and TX Pools

The decoder maintains two independent calibration pools:

PoolPurposeColour Tag
RX (Receive) Incoming Morse from another operator — audio inputs such as the pilot-tone mic or CW tone detector. RX text colour (green by default)
TX (Transmit) Morse you are sending — keyboard, mouse or touch keyer input. TX text colour (yellow by default)

Each pool tracks its own rolling average of dit/dah durations and derives its own WPM estimate independently. This is essential because the person you are working will almost certainly send at a different speed to you. If both speeds were mixed into a single pool, the adaptive thresholds would drift and produce decoding errors.

The main screen shows four WPM pill badges: RX, TX, Encoder and Keyer. You can click ▲/▼ to adjust the initial WPM for each, and use Reset RX Cal / Reset TX Cal to clear the accumulated calibration samples and restart auto-calibration from the current WPM setting.

2.3  Auto-Calibration

When a human operates a key (straight key or hand-sent paddle), element durations are never mathematically perfect. The auto-calibration algorithm measures each interval and, by dividing by the expected unit count (1 for dit, 3 for dah, etc.), estimates the current dit-unit length. A rolling average of these estimates converges on the sender's actual speed within a few characters.

Tip: Set the initial Decoder WPM reasonably close to the expected speed. The auto-calibrator works faster when it starts near the right value.

2.4  Perfect Timing (Keyer-Generated Elements)

When you use any of the iambic paddle modes (Iambic A, Iambic B, Ultimatic or Single Lever), the keyer generates dits and dahs with mathematically exact durations derived from the Keyer WPM setting. These perfect-timing elements must not be fed into the auto-calibration pool because their artificially precise durations would skew the adaptive averages that are meant to track natural human variation.

The application handles this automatically: when the iambic keyer produces an element, it sets a perfect-timing flag. The decoder then uses a fixed threshold calculated from the Keyer WPM instead of the adaptive threshold, and does not record any calibration samples. You do not need to configure anything — it just works.

Note: Straight-key input is always human-timed, even when sent from the keyboard, mouse or touch keyer. Straight-key events do feed into the calibration pool assigned to that input.

2.5  Fullscreen Decoder

Click the ⛶ button on the Morse Decoder panel to open a fullscreen view optimised for on-air operating. Decoded text is shown in large, customisable fonts. RX and TX text appear in different colours so you can follow the conversation at a glance.

The fullscreen decoder has its own independent text buffer. Clearing text in the fullscreen view does not affect the main decoder panel, and vice versa. Both buffers accumulate text continuously and survive closing and reopening the fullscreen modal — your conversation history is preserved until you explicitly clear the buffer.

Pressing the Clear button (🗑) opens a small context menu with two options: This text area clears only the current buffer, while All text areas clears all four display buffers at once (main decoder, main encoder, fullscreen decoder, fullscreen encoder). The encoder’s clear menu additionally offers a Text area option that empties the text-entry field without touching any conversation log.
